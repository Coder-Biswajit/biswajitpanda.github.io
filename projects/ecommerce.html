Overview

The E-Commerce Management System is a backend retail platform handling products, customers, and orders — with a strong emphasis on SQL-driven analytics. The goal was not just to manage data, but to make it tell a business story: which products drive the most revenue, who the highest-value customers are, and where sales trends are heading.

The system combines operational data management (CRUD, relational structure) with business intelligence (daily/monthly reports, customer segmentation, product ranking) — bridging engineering and analytics in one project.

Key Features
🛒
Full Order Management
Product catalog, customer records, and order processing — all managed through a relational MySQL schema with full CRUD operations.
🏆
Top Product Analysis
SQL queries identifying best-selling SKUs, revenue per category, and inventory movement patterns.
👤
Customer Segmentation
High-value customer identification using purchase frequency, order value, and recency analysis through SQL aggregates.
📅
Daily & Monthly Reports
Automated report generation via Python + Pandas, with Power BI dashboards visualizing revenue, order volume, and growth trends.
Technical Approach

The database schema was designed with normalization in mind — products, customers, orders, and order items as separate normalized tables connected by foreign keys. This structure enables efficient analytical queries without data redundancy.

Sales analysis queries use multi-table JOINs with GROUP BY and HAVING to rank products by revenue and segment customers by lifetime value. Output is piped through Pandas for date-range filtering and formatting, then loaded into Power BI for visual dashboards showing trends across daily and monthly granularities.

Learnings

This project taught me to think like a business analyst, not just a developer. Writing queries to answer "who are our best customers?" required me to define what "best" means in data terms — and that definition shaped the entire analysis.

Working with daily and monthly aggregations also reinforced the importance of time-based thinking in data analysis — trends are invisible without the right temporal granularity, and choosing the wrong window can hide or distort patterns.
